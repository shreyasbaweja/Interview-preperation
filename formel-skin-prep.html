Walk me through your CRO experience and biggest impact
+

CRO has been the constant thread through everything I've done in product. Over four years at Outfittery, I grew website funnel conversion by over 120% while keeping CAC in check — and the CAC part is what I'm most proud of, because growth that inflates your acquisition cost isn't really growth.

But I want to share how I approached it, because the process is what made it repeatable. I always started with data and cast a wide net — Tableau for step and funnel conversion, time spent on each page, Hotjar for heatmaps and scroll behaviour, session recordings to catch real drop-off moments. I'd also set up surveys at key touchpoints — exit surveys, post-completion surveys — and even used them to validate designs before a single line of code was written. When we were redesigning the success page and account dashboard, survey feedback shaped the direction entirely.

From there I'd develop rough wireframes and run lean feedback loops simultaneously with users, designers, and developers. Almost every decision had a back-of-envelope business case attached — not complex, but enough to pressure-test whether the effort was worth it.

Then we'd build with an MVP approach — defining the experimentation direction upfront, agreeing on which KPIs we expected to move, and building iteratively so we could keep learning. Never one big launch. Always continuous iteration.

That process delivered the homepage revamp — where the 'how it works' block turned out to be one of our biggest single conversion lifters. The signup page restructure where we added Google login and moved explanatory content below the fold. The subscription defaults change which became a meaningful investor story. And smaller wins like making the phone number field optional — immediate conversion lift, almost zero engineering effort.

Tell me about a time you worked cross-functionally and faced friction
+

The one that stands out most is when I proposed adding a new informative step in the funnel — one that I knew upfront was going to reduce short-term conversion. That's a hard pitch to make to a marketing team whose bonuses are tied to conversion and CAC.

The pushback was immediate: "Why would we deliberately hurt our conversion rate?" And honestly, that's a fair question. So rather than debating opinions, I brought everyone to the data. I showed Hotjar recordings of customers visibly confused at the payment step. Exit survey responses where people said they didn't feel confident about what they were paying for. Tableau data showing cancellation spikes right after the first order. The story was clear — we were converting customers who weren't ready, and we were paying for it downstream.

Then I built a simple business case — if we reduced cancellations by even a modest amount, the lifetime value gain would more than offset the conversion dip. That shifted the conversation from "you want to hurt my numbers" to "okay, how do we test this properly."

We aligned on a structured experiment — clear hypothesis, agreed KPIs, defined what success looked like beyond just the funnel. Built it iteratively so we could course-correct. And it worked. Cancellations dropped, AOV stayed stable, LTV improved.

But the thing I'm proudest of isn't the result — it's that it changed how that team thought about conversion. Not as a single funnel metric, but as a signal of business health.

How do you prioritise competing experiments and projects?
+

Prioritisation for me always starts with impact potential anchored to actual business goals — not gut feel, and definitely not whoever shouted loudest in the last meeting.

My starting point is always the funnel data. Where are the biggest drop-offs? Where is the most volume? A 5% improvement at a high-traffic step almost always beats a 20% improvement somewhere with low traffic. So I size the opportunity first before anything else.

Then I layer in effort — working with developers to get a realistic complexity read. That leads naturally into a rough ICE-style scoring: impact, confidence, ease. It gives me something objective to anchor stakeholder conversations on, so prioritisation doesn't turn into a political debate.

I also always try to maintain two parallel tracks. One for high-impact, higher-effort strategic bets — things like funnel restructuring or new steps. And one for quick wins — small changes like tweaking copy or making a field optional — that ship fast, generate learnings, and keep momentum alive while the bigger work is in progress.

The one thing I'm strict about is not running too many experiments simultaneously on overlapping parts of the funnel — because that corrupts your data and makes it impossible to attribute results cleanly. Statistical rigour isn't optional. It's foundational.

And prioritisation is a living thing — I revisit it regularly, because what was the right call three weeks ago may not be right today.

Coaching Notes
  • "Not whoever shouted loudest" lands with every HR person who's seen that happen
  • The two parallel tracks point shows you balance strategy with momentum — that's a senior PM skill
  • Statistical rigour point maps directly to their JD language — it will register

Tell me about a test that failed — what did you learn?
+

This one is genuinely one of my most important learning moments, so I'm happy to be honest about it.

We were running an experiment introducing a styling fee for womenswear customers — but due to a technical oversight, it accidentally went live for a small proportion of menswear customers too. It was low season, the sample was small, and the team decided to keep it running and observe rather than pull it immediately.

The early results were extraordinary. AOV showed a potential 50% uplift. Conversion drop was less than 20%. And the data was statistically significant at over 95% confidence. On paper this looked like a company-level growth opportunity. I got genuinely excited and strongly advocated for taking it further.

So we ran structured follow-up experiments to validate and scale it. And that's where it fell apart. Conversion declined far more than the initial test suggested, and the AOV uplift didn't hold. The opportunity I had championed simply didn't exist at scale.

The learning was humbling. Even statistically valid, high-confidence data can mislead you — especially when it came from an accidental experiment running during low season with unusual sample characteristics. Novelty effects, seasonal behaviour, small sample quirks — they can all make results look better than they really are.

Since then I've always been the person in the room saying: let's measure this over time, across seasons, and at scale before we commit. No matter how perfect the numbers look in the moment. The most dangerous test result is one that looks too good to be true.

Coaching Notes
  • Opening with "I'm happy to be honest about it" sets a confident, not defensive tone
  • The failure wasn't careless — you followed rigorous process and it still misled you. That's sophisticated
  • "Too good to be true" closer is memorable and shows real self-awareness
  • This differentiates you from candidates who pick trivial failures or can't recall any

How do you build a culture of experimentation?
+

This is something I care a lot about — because the biggest barrier to experimentation is almost never technical. It's behavioural. People are naturally loss-averse. They're afraid a test will hurt their numbers, or that a failed experiment will reflect badly on them personally. Breaking that mindset is the real work.

My approach has always come down to three things: democratise the data, celebrate the learning, and make it feel safe to test.

On data — I made sure that funnel performance and test results weren't locked inside product. I shared them actively with marketing, CRM, and performance teams. Ran sessions where I walked people through what the data was telling us and encouraged them to bring their own hypotheses. Over time, experimentation stopped being a product team activity and started happening across the business. More than 40 A/B tests, many co-owned by non-product teams.

On celebrating learning — when the styling fee experiment didn't hold up at scale, I wrote it up transparently and shared it widely. I framed it as exactly what experimentation is designed to do — catch something before you commit fully. That reframe matters enormously for psychological safety.

On making it safe — this comes back to process. When teams see that every experiment has a clear hypothesis, defined KPIs, and a structured rollback plan, it stops feeling like a gamble and starts feeling like a controlled investment in learning.

The goal is to reach a point where people feel more uncomfortable not testing than testing. That's the real culture shift. And it's what I'd want to build at FORMEL SKIN.

Coaching Notes
  • "Biggest barrier is behavioural not technical" immediately signals leadership maturity
  • Referencing the styling fee failure here ties your answers together coherently
  • "More uncomfortable not testing than testing" is memorable and quotable

How do you handle conflict with developers?
+

The honest answer is that I try to make conflict unlikely before it starts. Every ticket I write includes a business case, a clear definition of done, and a cost-of-delay calculation. When a developer understands why something matters commercially — not just what they're building — the conversation naturally shifts from "do we do this" to "how do we do this best." That removes a huge amount of friction upfront.

But conflict still happens, and when it does my position is clear. I deeply respect technical expertise — developers often see things I don't. So my first move is always to genuinely listen and understand the constraint. Sometimes they're right and I adjust. But if I believe the business case is strong, I ask them to make an equally strong case for why we shouldn't move forward — with data, not just effort estimates.

A real example: a developer pushed back on a funnel change because of technical debt — the existing architecture made it messier than it should have been. I understood that completely. But I showed what delay was costing us in conversion value every week we didn't ship. We found a middle path — an MVP implementation that wasn't architecturally perfect but delivered the business result, with a follow-up ticket committed for the cleanup. Both sides got what they needed.

My general principle is: I will never dismiss a technical concern, but I will always anchor the conversation back to business impact. Developers respond much better to a PM who shows them the numbers and gives them real context than one who just says "because the business wants it." Give them the why, and most of the time they become your strongest allies.

Coaching Notes
  • Opening with prevention shows maturity — you solve problems before they exist
  • The MVP middle path example shows pragmatism — you don't need to win every battle, just the outcome
  • The closing principle is genuine — it reflects how good PMs actually operate
