How do you prioritise competing experiments and projects?
+

Prioritisation always starts with impact potential anchored to actual business goals — not gut feel, and definitely not whoever shouted loudest in the last meeting.

My starting point is always the funnel data. Where are the biggest drop-offs? Where is the most volume? A 5% improvement at a high-traffic step almost always beats a 20% improvement at a low-traffic one. Size the opportunity first.

Then I layer in effort — working with developers to get a realistic complexity read. That leads into a rough ICE-style scoring: impact, confidence, ease. It gives me something objective to anchor stakeholder conversations on, so prioritisation doesn't become a political debate.

I also always maintain two parallel tracks. One for high-impact, higher-effort strategic bets — funnel restructuring, new steps. And one for quick wins — small changes like tweaking copy or making a field optional — that ship fast, generate learnings, and keep momentum alive while the bigger work is in progress.

The one thing I'm strict about: never running too many experiments simultaneously on overlapping funnel segments. That corrupts data and makes attribution impossible. Statistical rigour isn't optional — it's foundational.

Tell me about a test that failed — what did you learn?
+

This is genuinely one of my most important learning moments, so I'm happy to be honest about it.

We were running an experiment introducing a styling fee for womenswear customers — but due to a technical oversight, it accidentally went live for a small proportion of menswear customers too. It was low season, the sample was small, and the team decided to keep it running and observe.

The early results were extraordinary. AOV showed a potential 50% uplift. Conversion drop was less than 20%. Statistically significant at over 95% confidence. On paper this looked like a company-level opportunity. I got genuinely excited and strongly advocated for taking it further.

So we ran structured follow-up experiments to validate. And that's where it fell apart. Conversion declined far more than the initial test suggested, and the AOV uplift didn't hold. The opportunity I had championed simply didn't exist at scale.

The learning was humbling. Even statistically valid, high-confidence data can mislead you — especially when it came from an accidental experiment during low season with unusual sample characteristics. Novelty effects, seasonal behaviour, small sample quirks — they can all make results look better than they are. Since then I've always been the person in the room saying: let's measure this over time, across seasons, and at scale before we commit. The most dangerous test result is one that looks too good to be true.

How do you build a culture of experimentation?
+

The biggest barrier to experimentation is almost never technical. It's behavioural. People are naturally loss-averse. They're afraid a test will hurt their numbers, or that a failed experiment reflects badly on them. Breaking that mindset is the real work.

My approach has always come down to three things: democratise the data, celebrate the learning, and make it feel safe to test.

On data — I made sure that funnel performance and test results weren't locked inside product. I shared them actively with marketing, CRM, and performance teams, ran sessions walking people through what the data was telling us, and encouraged them to bring their own hypotheses. Over time experimentation stopped being a product team activity and started happening across the business. 40+ A/B tests, many co-owned by non-product teams.

On celebrating learning — when the styling fee experiment didn't hold up at scale, I wrote it up transparently and shared it widely. I framed it as exactly what experimentation is designed to do: catch something before you commit fully. That reframe matters enormously for psychological safety.

On making it safe — this comes back to process. When teams see that every experiment has a clear hypothesis, defined KPIs, and a structured rollback plan, it stops feeling like a gamble and starts feeling like a controlled investment in learning. The goal is a point where people feel more uncomfortable not testing than testing. That's the real culture shift.

How do you handle conflict with developers?
+

I try to make conflict unlikely before it starts. Every ticket I write includes a business case, a clear definition of done, and a cost-of-delay calculation. When a developer understands why something matters commercially — not just what they're building — the conversation shifts from "do we do this" to "how do we do this best." That removes a huge amount of friction upfront.

But conflict still happens. When it does, my first move is to genuinely listen and understand the technical constraint. Sometimes they're right and I adjust. But if the business case is strong, I ask them to make an equally strong case for why we shouldn't — with data, not just effort estimates.

A real example: a developer pushed back on a funnel change because of technical debt — the existing architecture made it messier than it should have been. I showed what delay was costing us in conversion value every week we didn't ship. We found a middle path — an MVP implementation that wasn't architecturally perfect but delivered the business result, with a follow-up ticket committed for cleanup. Both sides got what they needed.

My principle: I will never dismiss a technical concern, but I will always anchor back to business impact. Give developers the why, and most of the time they become your strongest allies.

Walk me through how you designed your experimentation framework from scratch — what were the hardest decisions?
+

At Outfittery I didn't inherit a framework — I built it from scratch over four years. The tooling was the easy part. The hardest decisions were all about governance.

The framework had five layers. First, a multi-signal data stack: Tableau for step-level funnel conversion, Hotjar for heatmaps and recordings, exit surveys at key drop-off points, and session recordings for real-time behavioural patterns. Four sources not one — because each source reveals a different dimension of the same problem. Second, a hypothesis system anchored to funnel volume, not just conversion delta. A 5% lift at a high-traffic step almost always outweighs a 20% lift at a low-traffic one. Third, a test design protocol: primary KPI plus guardrail metrics defined before any code was written. No test without a written hypothesis. Fourth, governance: maximum two concurrent tests on overlapping funnel segments. And fifth, a knowledge system where every test — including failures — was documented, shared, and framed as a learning investment.

The hardest decision was enforcing the governance rule. We had stakeholders who wanted five tests running simultaneously. I had to hold the line — because when two tests run on overlapping segments without proper segregation, you can't attribute results. I used a real example where two tests collided and we lost three weeks of usable data. That argument stuck.

At Voy, with the questionnaire → plan selection → payment funnel, the same discipline applies. Test contamination in a health context doesn't just cost data quality — it could influence clinical perception.

Framework at a Glance Signals → Hypothesis System → Test Protocol → Governance (max 2 concurrent overlapping) → Knowledge System (failures documented publicly). The hardest layer is always governance — cultural before technical.

How do you prevent p-hacking or premature conclusions in your team?
+

Four non-negotiable rules:

1. Pre-register everything. Hypothesis, primary KPI, MDE, and end date — all written before any test goes live. If it wasn't written first, it doesn't count as a validated result.

2. No mid-test peeking. We use sequential testing methods if interim reads are genuinely needed — not standard significance thresholds on live data.

3. Duration is locked. The test ends on the pre-agreed date, not when the numbers look good. I've had stakeholders ask me to call tests early three times. Answered no three times.

4. High-stakes results require replication. One test result is a hypothesis. We validate before we scale — and before we build investor narrative around it.

Use the Styling Fee Story Here The most dangerous test result I ever saw looked perfect: 95% confidence, 50%+ AOV uplift. The data was statistically valid. It was also from an accidental low-season experiment on an unintended segment. We scaled it — and it collapsed. Even a clean result from the wrong conditions is a misleading result. That experience is why I treat every statistically significant result as a hypothesis until replicated across seasons and at scale. The styling fee experiment is now my team's shorthand for not celebrating too early.

If the data says one thing and your gut says another — what do you do?
+

Never give a binary answer. The interesting answer is in the diagnostic.

Interrogate the gut first. Intuition is usually a pattern match. "This feels wrong" typically means "I've seen this situation fail before." Ask: is the pattern actually relevant here, or am I anchoring on the wrong prior experience?

Interrogate the data second. Is the sample large enough? Was the test run for enough duration? Is there a segment or time-period effect hiding in the average?

If both survive scrutiny — design a third test. Specifically designed to arbitrate the conflict. Don't flip a coin and don't default to data just because data sounds rigorous. Rigour is about asking the right questions, not deferring to numbers.

My honest scorecard: The styling fee story is my proof that gut instinct can be wrong — the data looked right, I advocated hard for it, and it collapsed at scale. But in other cases — seasonal distortion, novelty effects, traffic mix shifts — intuition has saved us from calling a test too early. Neither source has a monopoly on truth. The skill is knowing which one deserves more weight in a given situation.
